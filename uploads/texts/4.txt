Project Evaluation
A higher-level assessment of the project to see whether it is
worthwhile to proceed with the project.
1. Strategic Assessment
• To see whether the project will fit in the strategic planning of the whole
organization.
2. Technical Assessment
• To determine whether it is desirable to carry out the development and
operation of the software system.
3. Economical Assessment
• To decide which of the several alternative projects has a better success
rate and a high turnover.

Benefit Map
• A Benefit Map is a visual tool used in project evaluation and business
analysis to show how a project’s activities and outcomes lead to
strategic benefits for the organization.
• A Benefit Map links together:
• Project Activities/Changes
• What you're doing in the project (e.g., build new software/feature)
• Enablers/Capabilities
• What the project enables (e.g., faster data processing).
• Business Benefits
• Tangible effects (e.g., reduced processing time, improved data accuracy).
• Intermediate Outcomes
• Organizational value (e.g., cost savings).
• Strategic Objectives (Long-term goal)

Technical Assessment
• Identifies the functions that the software is expected to perform.
• Evaluate whether the required functionality can be achieved with
current or affordable technologies.
• Consider the organizational policy on providing technical
infrastructure.
• Align with the SISP (Strategic Information System Plan) of the
organization.
• A Comprehensive document that bridges the technology with business
strategy. It defines how information systems (software/IT systems) will
help the organization to achieve its goals over the coming years.

Explanation of Life Cycle Cash Flow
• Initial Investment Phase (first downward curve)
• Shows negative cash flow as money is spent on development, equipment, marketing,
and launch costs. This is when you're putting money into the project before seeing
returns.
• Growth Phase (upward curve)
• Cash flow becomes positive as the product gains market acceptance and sales
increase. Revenue starts exceeding ongoing costs.
• Maturity Phase (peak of the curve)
• Represents maximum profitability when the product has achieved optimal market
penetration and efficiency.
• Decline Phase (downward slope)
• Shows decreasing cash flow as sales decline due to market saturation, competition, or
product obsolescence.
• End-of-Life Phase
• Represent costs associated with product discontinuation rather than value recovery

ROI (Return On Investment)
• Return on Investment (ROI) is a financial metric used to evaluate
the efficiency or profitability of an investment.
• It tells you how much return you’re getting compared to the cost of
the investment.
• Expressed as a percentage (%).
• Highest ROI will be selected.
• ROI = (Net Profit / Investment Cost) x 100.
• Measuring ROI is critical before starting any software project.

ROI (Return On Investment)
• Example
Development Cost: $30,000
Expected annual savings in employee time: $20,000
Tool lifespan: 3 years
Total Benefit = 3×20,000=60,000
ROI = (60,000 – 30,000) / 30,000 x 100 = 100%
So, you’d get your money back and double the investment over 3 years.
