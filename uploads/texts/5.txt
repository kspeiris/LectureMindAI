What is a risk?
• An unforeseen/unexpected event may or may not occur in the future that can
adversely impact the project’s cost, schedule, or quality.
• Relates to the future (Risk is future-oriented).
• Measured in terms of probability of occurrences and consequences.
• Involves cause and effect.
• Every risk has a cause (why it might happen) and a potential effect (what might happen if it
does).
• 2 types of risks
• Positive Risks (Opportunities)
• Negative Risks (Threats)

Risk Estimation
• Risk Exposure
• Expected value of the loss for the risk.
• Risk Likelihood
• The probability of the risk
• Risk Impact
• The total loss incurred if the risk happens.

Risk Planning
• How to deal with risks?
• Risk Acceptance (Tolerate)
• Do nothing. Live with the risk because it's small, or fixing it costs too much.
• Example: Not buying phone insurance for an old phone.
• Risk Avoidance (Terminate)
• Don't do the risky activity at all.
• Example: Not driving to avoid car accidents.
• Risk Reduction (Treat)
• Make the risk smaller or less harmful.
• Example: Wearing a helmet when biking.
• RRL
• Risk Transfer
• Transfer risk to another party
• Insurance, Outsourcing

Evaluate the effects of
uncertainty
• Techniques to consider the uncertainties in the duration of
activities within a project.
• PERT (Program Evaluation Review Technique)
• E = (O + 4M + P) / 6
• S = (P – O) / 6
• E – Expected Duration
• O – Optimistic Time
• P – Pessimistic Time
• M – Most Likely Time
• S – Standard Deviation
• Monte Carlo / Probability simulation
• Critical Chain Management
Madusanka Nipunajith 21
Evaluate the effects of
uncertainty
• These values are NOT calculated mathematically at the start.
• They are:
• Collected from experts
• Based on historical data
• Or team discussion/judgment
• So PERT is a probabilistic estimation method, not a fixed
formula system.
• Once O, M, and P are given, we calculate - Expected time (TE)
• This gives a weighted average, where:
• Most likely (M) has the highest weight
• Pessimistic and optimistic contribute less

PERT CHART
PERT Chart and AON are closely related, but they are not the same thing.
PERT
• Answers - How long might each activity take under uncertainty?
• Uses optimistic, most likely, and pessimistic time estimates
• Focuses on uncertainty in activity durations
• Can be drawn as a network diagram (AOA)
AON
• Answers - In what order should activities be performed, and when can they start and finish?
• Usually uses a single duration estimate
• Focuses on activity relationships and scheduling
• Is itself a network diagram
Madusanka Nipunajith 26
PERT CHART – Historical Difference
• Originally
• PERT used Activity-on-Arrow (AOA) networks.
• Modern project management software (MS Project, Primavera, etc.) typically
uses Activity-on-Node (AON) networks.
• Therefore
• Many textbooks today combine the ideas and say:
• "Draw a PERT chart."
• Drawing an AON network with PERT time estimates.
• PERT is the technique used to estimate uncertain activity durations, while AON is
the network diagram used to represent activities and their dependencies. In
practice, PERT calculations are often performed on an AON diagram.
Madusanka Nipunajith 27
Pros & Cons of PERT
• Pros
• Better Time Estimation
• PERT uses three time estimates (optimistic, pessimistic, and most likely), giving a
more realistic and flexible schedule.
• Identifies Critical Path
• Helps managers identify the critical path, showing the task sequence that directly
affects project duration.
• Helps in Risk Management
• By considering uncertainties in time estimates, risks become visible before project
execution, and potential delays can be addressed proactively.
• Supports Decision-Making
• Provides useful data for what-if analysis and scenario planning in uncertain
environments.
Madusanka Nipunajith 28
Pros & Cons of PERT
• Cons
• Time-Consuming to Create
• Developing a detailed PERT chart with multiple time estimates for each activity can
be complex and slow.
• Requires Accurate Data
• PERT relies on subjective estimates. Inaccurate inputs can lead to misleading results.
• Not Ideal for Short Projects
• For small or routine projects, PERT might be overkill, adding unnecessary complexity.
• Difficult to Update
• Once a project starts, updating the PERT chart as changes happen can be difficult
and time-consuming.
